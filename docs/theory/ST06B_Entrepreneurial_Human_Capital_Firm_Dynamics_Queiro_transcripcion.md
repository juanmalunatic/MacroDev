# Summary of Results

Firms started by entrepreneurs with 15 or more years of schooling vs six or less:

- 39% larger at entry in terms of sales
- 2.7 times larger by age 10.
- Pareto upper tail of syze distribution is 1.09 vs 1.56.
- Most of this differential growth occurs within the first five years of the life cycle
- not explained by selection into particular sectors, and by survivor growth
- specific to entrepreneurs. The average schooling of other workers appears to matter much less for firm dynamics.
- ability bias plays a limited role

# Aggregate Implications

- Aggregate returns to schooling: sum of individual mean returns in employment and in entrepreneurship
- Effect on TFP not captured by estimates of human capital externalities that rely on differences in wage levels, housing costs or the average revenue product of inputs across locations
- Differences in firm productivity in the model are reflected in firm size rather than these factors.
- Mincerian regressions understate the contribution of individual returns in each occupation.
- fraction of cross-country income variation that can be explained by human and physical capital increases from the 40% reported by Caselli (2005) to between 59% and 78%.

## Slide 17 / 27

# Model

Final Good Sector:

$$
Y = \left(\int y(\omega)^{\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma}} d(\omega)\right)^{\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1}}
$$

$$
y(\omega) = Y \left(\frac{p(\omega)}{P}\right)^{-\sigma}
$$

- Measure one of agents born and die at exogenous rate \(\delta\). Endowed with schooling level \(s\) at birth with probability \(\theta_s\)
- Productivity:
  - \(e^h\) when working as employee
  - \(e^z\) when working as entrepreneur

$$
h = rs + \varepsilon, \quad r \sim N(\bar r, \sigma_r^2), \quad \varepsilon \sim N(0, \sigma_\varepsilon^2)
$$

$$
z = \nu s + \eta, \quad \nu \text{ is constant}, \quad \eta \sim N(0, \sigma_\eta^2)
$$

$$
\dot z = \mu_0 + \mu_1 s
$$

- \(\dot z\) becomes zero at Poisson rate \(m\).

## Slide 18 / 27

# Firms

$$
y = e^z K_z^\gamma H_z^{1-\gamma}
$$

$$
\pi(z) = \max_{p,K_z,H_z} \left(p e^z K_z^\gamma H_z^{1-\gamma} - \rho K_z - wH_z\right)
$$

As a result:

$$
\pi(z) \propto p(z)y(z) \propto H_z \propto e^{(\sigma-1)z}
$$

and

$$
\kappa = K_z/H_z = \frac{\gamma}{1-\gamma}\frac{w}{\rho}.
$$

- Threshold \(z^*(h)\) such that \(\pi(z^*) = we^h\)
- Choosing the threshold \(z_0^*\) for an agent with \(h = 0\) as the normalizing threshold,

$$
z^* = z_0^* + \frac{h}{\sigma-1}
$$

## Slide 19 / 27

# Equilibrium

- Labor market clearing determines \(w\) and

$$
z_0^* = \frac{\ln\left[(\sigma - 1)(1-\gamma)\frac{Z^*}{H^*}\right]}{\sigma - 1}
$$

where

$$
Z^* = E\left[e^{(\sigma-1)z}\mid z \ge z^*\right]Pr(z \ge z^*)
$$

and

$$
H^* = E\left[e^h \mid z < z^*\right]Pr(z < z^*)
$$

- Given a stationary distribution of productivity, a steady-state equilibrium consists of \((z_0^*, \kappa, \pi, w)\), such that:
  - occupational choices maximize income
  - firms maximize profits and
  - labour market clears.

## Slide 20 / 27

# Stationary Distribution

- The distribution of the level of productivity of households \(\exp(z)\), has right Pareto tail with coefficient:

$$
\alpha_s = \frac{\delta + m}{\mu_0 + \mu_1 s}
$$

- \(\alpha_s\) is the parameter of the exponential distribution of cummulative increments from initial \(z\).
- Distribution of firm productivity combines this distribution with entrepreneurial choice
  - For large \(z\), the fraction of active entrepreneurs converges to one, so the right tail of the distribution of firm productivity \(\exp(z)\) is also Pareto with index \(\alpha_s\).
  - Impact of schooling dependent on parameter \(\mu_1\)

## Slide 21 / 27

# Firm Dynamics

- Firm productivity grows at rate \(\mu_0 + \mu_1 s\) until it stops.
- Expected cumulative growth for a given cohort will converge to \(\frac{\mu_0+\mu_1 s}{m}\), implies:

$$
E(z \mid s,a) = E(z \mid s, a = 0) + \frac{\mu_0 + \mu_1 s}{m}\left(1 - e^{-ma}\right)
$$

- Each cohort has two type of entrepreneurs: consumers born with \(z \ge z^*\) and those that join later. Distribution rather difficult to derive.

# Estimation

- Parameters set a priori: \(\sigma = 3\) (Hsieh and Klenow), \(\gamma = 1/3\) and \(\bar r = 8\%\), midpoint of returns to schooling estimates.
- Tail index of size distribution for each schooling: \(\alpha_s/(\sigma - 1)\) identifies \(\alpha_s\) (does it for top and bottom levels of schooling, uses formulas to back out the rest)
- \(\alpha_s\) is sufficient statistic for the effect of schooling on aggregate output through productivity growth
  - Any combination of \(\delta, m, \mu_0\) and \(\mu_1\) that is consistent with \(\alpha_s\) leads to same aggregate implications

## Slide 25 / 27

# Implications for Development Accounting

- In standard models of development accounting reviewed in class (e.g. Klenow and Rodriguez-Clare),

$$
Y = AH
$$

where \(h = e^{rs}\) is average schooling and \(r\) is rate of return to schooling

- In this paper, \(A\) is also a function of schooling:

$$
\frac{\partial \ln Y}{\partial s}
= \frac{1}{1-\gamma}\frac{\partial \ln A}{\partial s}
+ \frac{\partial \ln H}{\partial s}
$$

- Ignoring selection effects, the effect or human capital on TFP is proportional to return to schooling in entrepreneurship:

$$
\frac{\partial \ln A}{\partial s}
= \frac{1}{\sigma - 1}\frac{\partial \ln E\left[e^{(\sigma-1)z}\right]}{\partial s}
= \frac{1}{\sigma - 1}\frac{\partial \ln E[\pi(z)]}{\partial s}
$$

- Baseline estimate for the geometric mean return equals 10.9%. The arithmetic mean, which determines \(\frac{\partial \ln A}{\partial s}\), equals 15.6% for an entrepreneur with six years of schooling
- Implies aggregate returns to schooling for a population with \(s = 6\) equal 22.8%, with 11.7% coming from the effect of entrepreneurial human capital on TFP.
